BM1370 PLL Tuning Spreadsheet
This spreadsheet maps every real achievable frequency for the BM1370 ASIC — the chip inside the Bitaxe Gamma and several other Bitaxe variants — based directly on pll_table.h from the ESP-Miner source code.
It exists because AxeOS doesn't tell you the truth about frequency.
When you type a whole number into the AxeOS frequency field — 770, 735, 600 — the firmware echoes your input back unchanged. It looks like it worked. But the BM1370's Phase-Locked Loop can only produce a finite set of discrete frequencies determined by integer mathematics. Your whole number almost certainly isn't one of them. The PLL snapped to the nearest real step silently, and AxeOS never told you.
This spreadsheet shows you what those real steps are, and helps you confirm which one you're actually running on.

What's in the spreadsheet
Every row is a real frequency pulled directly from pll_table.h. These are not estimates or calculations — they are the exact values the BM1370 PLL can actually produce.
Clean 6.25 MHz multiples are flagged with ✓ — these are the recommended operating points for most users. They are exactly representable in the firmware's floating point arithmetic, which means what you type into AxeOS is what you get back. The confirmation is simple: if AxeOS echoes back a decimal value rather than a whole number, you're on a real step.
The gold highlight shows the nearest valid PLL step to whatever frequency you currently have set. This is your starting point — it tells you where you probably are right now.
Green input cells are where you enter your stock AxeOS values — your current frequency, voltage, hashrate, power, ASIC temp, and VR temp. The spreadsheet uses these as your baseline.
Columns L-P are an observation log. As you test each frequency step, record what you see — hashrate, error rate, temperatures, wattage. Over time this builds your personal efficiency curve for your specific chip.
Hash per clock cycle and capacitive load are calculated columns carried over from Dep's original spreadsheet (see acknowledgements). Their interpretation is beyond the scope of this document.

How to use it
Step 1 — Enter your current AxeOS values in the green cells
Use whatever your dashboard is showing right now. This is your baseline.
Step 2 — Look at the gold highlight
This is the nearest real PLL step to your current frequency setting. If you've been entering whole numbers, this is likely where your chip has actually been running — not the number you typed.
Step 3 — Identify your target frequency
Look for the ✓ rows — clean 6.25 MHz multiples. Pick one close to where you want to operate. Type that value exactly into AxeOS — for example, 762.5 not 762. Save it.
Step 4 — Confirm the round-trip
Check what AxeOS reports back. If it shows a decimal — 762.5 MHz — you're confirmed on a real PLL step. If it echoes back a whole number unchanged, add decimal places and try again.
Step 5 — Log your observations in columns L-P
Run at that frequency for at least 20 minutes before noting anything down. Record hashrate, error rate, temperatures, wattage. Move to the next step only when you have stable data.

Important — read this before you start
This spreadsheet is a precision tuning tool. It assumes your system is already stable. It will not fix thermal problems or voltage instability — it will just map them more precisely.
Before this spreadsheet is useful to you:

Your cooling must be adequate — heatsink on the ASIC, heatsink on the VR, heatsink near the power jack, and airflow across the board
Your voltage must be stable at your chosen frequency — if your error rate is all over the place, sort voltage first
Your VR temperature must be within a range you are comfortable running at continuously

If those conditions aren't met, close the spreadsheet and address the hardware first. The frequency precision this tool provides only matters once everything underneath it is solid.
